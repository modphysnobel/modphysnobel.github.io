## 13.3. Steps in the cognitive development of high energy physics.

Previous sections described the content of a selection of Physics Nobel Prizes explicitly related to nuclear and particle physic, with few references to astrophysics and cosmology because those issues will be considered in next Chapter 14. The activities and results recognized with those awards show how the initial interest for producing nuclear reactions was transformed into the military and scientific convenience for understanding and controlling particle reactions. In sections 13.1 and 13.2 we have commented a total of 54 Nobel Prizes, corresponding 47 to Physics and 7 to Chemistry; they refer to the period starting in 1901 and ending in 2020. This information was summarized in Tables 13.1 and 13.2 where we indicated the year of the Prize, the name of the laureates and the reasons for its recognition (the subject indicating the awarded contribution). 

In this section we consider the same 54 Nobel Prizes organized according to the year of the discovery not the year when the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences announced the Nobel Prize. To define the year of each discovery we have consulted the document *Facts*, one of the documents describing each Nobel Prize in the Nobel Foundation page. Such dates indicate the moments when the laureates made their critical contributions by introducing new concepts, making important discoveries, applying revolutionary experimental procedures, proposing critical models, or creating new instruments or devices. 

In what follows we describe when and in what subjects the future laureates started working. We constated that only on few occasions the Nobel Prize has been awarded the next year of the discovery. There is a natural delay in between these two dates because any research project needs time and human and financial resources to be planned, developed, and communicated. Furthermore, the Nobel Committees require to analyze that the laureated works have opened promising tracks for fruitful investigations and applications. The description of these contributions constitutes a sort of a nonlinear descriptive network showing the cognitive development of high energy physics. The time line of these contributions, not the sequency of the Nobel Prize nominations, show the conceptual development of ideas, problems, and results under some historical perspective

The date indicating the beginning of the cognitive development of high energy physics corresponds to 1895 when Roentgen started working on those discharges in gases that later lead him to discover X-rays (Nobel Prize awarded in 1901). We close this cognitive development with the announcement in 2012 that two experiments conducted at the CERN laboratory confirmed the existence of the Higgs particle (Nobel Prize awarded in 2013). These are the lower and upper bounds of a community learning period 1895-2012 whose development will be divided into nine steps. This information is concentrated in Tables 13.3 to 13.11 where the laureate activities are classified according to three categories: the realization of experiments (marked in red), the creation of instruments (marked in green) and the elaboration of theories (marked in blue). 

When the work reported in any of these nine tables was made in collaboration with other scientists who were not mentioned in the Prize citation, then we underline the name of the Nobel laureate. Specific contributions that were not recognized with a Nobel Prize but involved some laureate works are written in boldface. For comparisons, the corresponding year when the Nobel Prize was announced is indicated as [N: year].

STEP 1: At the end of XIX Century several physicists had serious doubts about the material existence of atoms; at the most they assumed that the concept atom implied something indivisible, indistinguishable, and indestructible (see Table 13.3). However, some chemists were concerned about the structure of matter because the periodic table of elements was a useful scheme for organizing groups of elements; but the criteria of classification was not very well understood. Furthermore, electromagnetic radiations were described as continuous waves and there were no reasons for considering energy in terms of discrete values. New phenomena were observed and there were no possibilities for understanding them in the frame of classical physics. This was the case of experiments related to the discharges of gases in vacuum tubes, the appearance of radioactivity and the existence of an electronically charged particle inside the atom: the electron.

STEP 2: Further experimental investigations of radioactivity provoked a transformation in the structure of the atomic model by admitting the existence of two components: a positively charged heavy nucleus and a collection of surrounding electrons (see Table 13.4). This model served to understand the transformation of radioactive elements, the discovery of new elements and the existence of isotopes. However, that model did not explain the stability of atoms. Classically the atomic structure will be collapsed due to the dissipation of energy radiated by the electrons when they move in circular orbits. Furthermore, the registration of the tracks created by the passage of particles indicated the presence of subatomic particles under conditions of high energies and short times.

STEP 3: The interactions of wave like electromagnetic radiations with matter, as in the photoelectric effect and in the Compton effect, were explained in terms of quantized packages of energy: the photons (see Table 13.5). So, in a sort of symmetrical reciprocity condition, the corpuscular characteristic of particles like the electrons required a wave like description to make possible the production of observable diffraction patterns. Otherwise, due to the increased precision in the measurement of atomic spectra and the detailed calculations of the electronic energy levels, an appropriate interpretation of their results imposed an external restriction on the quantum numbers of the electrons with the same energy levels: Pauli exclusion principle.

STEP 4: Designing and building huge accelerators of particles and creating appropriate detection instruments transformed the way of doing research in physics (see Table 13.6). It strongly depended on technological developments and on the collaborative work of teams of scientists, engineers, and technicians. Understanding the nucleus models and discovering new particles like neutrons and positrons required the consideration of new types of forces, aside gravitation and electromagnetism: the strong force for explaining the nuclear binding between protons and neutrons (existence of mesons) and the weak force responsible of radioactivity. New theories started to be developed for explaining nuclear phenomena in the scale of stars and galaxies or for applying fundamental symmetry principles. The artificial production of radioactivity opened new possibilities for the transformation of elements and for the liberation of energy.

STEP 5: Preparation for World War Two oriented many research projects and after the war made possible the creation of experimental devices based on understanding specific aspects of the processes that produced the atomic bomb and the radar (see Table 13.7). Fission of heavy nuclei was produced first as a laboratory experiment and later as a political demonstration of force with the intention of ending the war by destroying two Japanese cities. Better understanding of nuclear processes accompanied the invention of new devices for detecting particles and determining nuclear properties like for instance, the existence of magic numbers in a nuclear shell model or the cycles of energy production in stars. Experiments and theories on quantum electrodynamics made possible understanding the quantized interaction of energy and matter.

STEP 6: Progress and problems concerning the use of nuclear energy were accompanied by the development of theories for a more complete explanation of nuclear structures (see Table 13.8). The following developments were possible: the creation of observation tools like bubble chambers, the application of experimental methods such as neutron spectroscopy and electron scattering, the presentation of evidence about the existence of neutrinos and antiprotons, and the formulation of theories concerning the formation of elements in the stars. 

On the side of particle physics, the eightfold way was proposed as an organizational scheme for classifying subatomic particles. This scheme included the development of the quark model according to which observed particles are composed by smaller building blocks with intrinsic properties such as: electric charge, mass, color charge and spin. The assumption that physical laws are characterized by symmetry operations, like replacement of matter by antimatter and reversion of left and right, was questioned in phenomenon such as the decay of certain elementary particles. The theory that the left-right symmetry law is violated by the weak interaction was confirmed by measuring the direction of motion of electrons during a cobalt isotope’s beta decay.

STEP 7: The Standard Model is a quantum field theory that refers to elementary particles and interaction fields (see Table 13.9). There are four fundamental forces in nature that result from the exchange of boson particles that work as force-carrier of the corresponding interaction field: the photons for the electromagnetic force which keep atoms together, the gluons for the strong force which holds atomic nuclei and quarks together, the W and Z particles for the weak force which is responsible for radioactive decay, and the gravitons for the gravitational force which acts on any object with mass or energy. 

The Standard Model is a theoretical framework for describing previous three forces, except for gravity. It also explains the structure of the elementary particles that form two basic building blocks of matter: the quarks and the leptons. The quarks are six (up, down, top, bottom, charm and strange) and they combine to form composite particles called hadrons like protons, neutrons, and mesons. There are six leptons; they cannot be broken down into further constituent particles: the electron and the electron neutrino, the muon and the muon neutrino, the tau and the tau neutrino.

The Standard Model unifies weak and electromagnetic interactions which combines into the electroweak force. The model also considers the existence of a fifth field created by the Higgs boson, that pervades all the space. Elementary particles acquire their mass by interacting with the Higgs field. When a particle moves inside this field, it experiences a resistance whose intensity is as high as the speed of its motion; this corresponds to a higher mass as a quantitative measure of inertia.

Outside this model, theoretical works started to be developed in connection with symmetry violations and relativistic considerations of the existence of black holes. Furthermore, relevant properties were discovered by using X-ray and neutrino spectroscopies.

STEP 8: The Standard Model was experimentally accepted when some of their predictions were confirmed and lead to the discovery of new particles (see Table 13.10). Furthermore, the results of deep inelastic scattering of electrons on protons and bound neutrons served to confirm that protons and neutrons are composed of quarks. In the discovery of the W and Z bosons the technique of stochastic cooling was important. The technique was developed at CERN with the purpose of compressing a beam of charged particles for having less energy spread and less angular divergence. Consequently, the particles are better organized, and the beam is “cooled” in the sense that its disorder is reduced. 
 
Concerning theoretical advances for overcoming limitations of the Standard Model, the mathematical structure of electroweak interaction was proposed and the study of the strong interaction between quarks and gluons was formulated. This theory is called Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) as an extension of the quark theory of strongly interacting hadronic particles. A property of this theory is asymptotic freedom which implies that interactions between particles become asymptotically weaker as the energy scale increases. This fact has two consequences: perturbative calculations are feasible because quarks interact weakly at high energies and quarks and gluons stay together because their interaction becomes strong at low energies.

STEP 9: Last thirty years have shown the development of experimental projects that have been developed during decades and involved more than thousand collaborators: the discovery of the W an Z quantized carriers of the weak interaction, the registration of a black hole, the oscillations of neutrinos and the existence of the boson Higgs (see Table 13.11).

In 1976 Carlo Rubbia had the idea to convert an existent large accelerator into a storage ring for protons and antiprotons, and Simon Van der Meer invented a method for dense packing and storage of protons, now applied for antiprotons. The first collisions in the CERN superaccelerator were observed in 1981; the discovery of the W and Z particles was announced in 1983 and recognized one year later by the Nobel Prize. 

In 1964 Roger Penrose indicated that the formation of black holes was predicted by Einstein in his general theory of relativity and developed the mathematical tools for describing the phenomenon. Since the 1990s Reinhard Genzel and Andrea Ghez developed techniques for studying the movements of stars in the middle of the Milky Way; their observations and calculations revealed the existence of a super massive black hole. The three physicists were awarded with the Nobel Prize in 2020.

In 1998 Takaaki Kajita detected neutrinos created in reactions between cosmic rays and the Earth’s atmosphere and in 2000 Arthur McDonald registered neutrinos created in nuclear reactions in the sun. Their measurements were made by a great number of detectors located in the interior of mines, one in Japan and another in Canada. Both received the Nobel Prize in 2015 "for the discovery of neutrino oscillations, which shows that neutrinos have mass".

In 1964 Peter Higgs and the team of François Englert and Robert Brout proposed independently a theory about the existence of a particle that explains why other particles have a mass.  In 2012, two experiments conducted at the CERN laboratory confirmed the existence of the Higgs particle. The 2013 Physics Nobel prize was awarded to Englert and Higgs.
